\begin{frame}{Water in Kenya}
    Article 42 of the Constitution of Kenya, 2010:
    \textcolor{msred}{“Every person has the right to a clean and
    healthy environment”}, while Article43 (d) of the Constitution
    guarantees the:
    \textcolor{msred}{“Right to clean and safe water in adequate
    quantities to every person.”}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Some
    numbers~\footnote[frame]{\tiny\url{https://eng.mst.dk/about-the-danish-epa/global-cooperation/kenyacircular-economy/kenya-water}}}
    \begin{itemize}

        \item<1-> Water available annually per person is about 452 cubic
            meters -  the UN threshold is 1000\footnote[frame]{\tiny
            1000-1700: stress, 500-1000: scarcity, 500: absolute scarcity}

        \item<2-> Of 54 million Kenyans,
            73\% percent have access to safe drinking water

        \item<3-> 40\% does not have access to safe water within
            1 km or max 30 min. walk.

    \end{itemize}
    \pause
    By 2050 the popluation is prognosed to double and water available per
    person could be halved.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Water in Kenya}
    Kenya generates between \textcolor{msred}{3,000 to 4,000 tons
    of waste per day.}
    \vspace{1em}

    Nairobi generates between
    \textcolor{msred}{
    2,000 to 2,500 tons of waste daily.}
    \vspace{1em}

    Less than \textcolor{msred}{half of that amount is re- or upcycled.}

    Dumped in: landfills, along roads, in streams or rivers or burned.
    \vspace{1em}

    This happens with little awareness of the subsurface and thus
    groundwater.\footnote[frame]{\footnotesize\url{https://eng.mst.dk/about-the-danish-epa/global-cooperation/kenyacircular-economy}}

\end{frame}
